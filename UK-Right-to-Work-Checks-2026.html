The Right to Work Regime is Expanding. Are You Ready?

Section 48 of the Border Security Act widens the rules beyond traditional employment. Contractors, agency staff, and platform workers are now your responsibility. Establish your digital statutory excuse today.

Get Protected with IRS HR Read the Action Plan

Do not wait until October 2026. Businesses using contingent labour, contractors, or agency staff must start mapping arrangements now.

What is Changing?

The baseline rule remains: check before employment starts to gain a "statutory excuse". But Section 48 adds two massive shifts to the landscape.

1

Wider Definition of "Employer"

The duty to check, and liability for failures, now applies to businesses engaging individuals through:

  • Worker's Contracts: Where personal service is required.
  • Sub-contractors: Individual contractors in a contractual chain.
  • Online Platforms: Matching services matching providers for a fee.
2

Introduction of "Extended Liability"

You can now be held liable for illegal working further down the supply chain, even with no direct contract with the worker.

If the direct employer (e.g., an agency) cannot be identified or failed their checks, the Home Office will look upstream.

You only get a statutory excuse against extended liability if you enforce strict contractual, substitution, and identity requirements on your suppliers.

High-Risk Areas for Businesses

Agency & Temp Workers

Using agency staff to deliver a service to your own customer? You are part of a contractual chain. The new extended liability provisions are designed to address exactly this.

A simple assurance from an agency that "checks are done" is no longer enough. You need a proper compliance framework and contractual protections that allow enforcement and audit rights.

The "Self-Employed" Label Trap

Do not assume a "contractor" label puts an engagement outside the scheme. The Home Office looks at reality, not just the contract. Ask yourself:

Must they do the work personally?
How does substitution actually work?
Are they integrated into your workforce?
Do they bear commercial risk?

Online Matching Platforms

If you operate a platform matching service providers to clients for a fee, the platform itself can now be treated as the employer. Gig-economy operators must integrate RtW checks into onboarding immediately.

Sponsor Licence Holders

This isn't just about civil penalties. Sponsors must retain evidence of checks for all workers (sponsored or not). Failure creates separate, severe sponsor compliance issues on top of illegal working fines.

Your 7-Step Action Plan

Don't wait for the October deadline. Implement these changes now to safeguard your business.

1

Map Your Workforce

Identify everyone carrying out work. Categorise them: employee, agency, sub-contractor, gig worker. Note where you are a pure end-user vs part of a supply chain.

2

Extend Your Processes

Right to work checks are no longer just for employees. Build checks into onboarding for workers, individual sub-contractors, and platform service providers.

3

Review Supplier Contracts

Flow-down requirements. Contracts must require right to work checks, prevent unapproved subcontracting, permit audits, and allow termination for illegal working.

4

Deal with Substitution

If a worker can send a substitute, you must ensure the substitute is checked *before* work starts. Responsibility cannot simply be delegated to the original worker.

5

Check Identity Throughout

Maintain systems (access passes, attendance systems, shift re-verification) to ensure the person doing the work is the exact person you checked initially.

6

Train the Wider Team

This affects more than HR. Procurement, site managers, and contract managers need to understand the rules and why a "casual assurance" from a supplier is dangerous.

7

Keep Checks Consistent

Avoid unlawful discrimination. Do not check only those who "appear" to be migrants or make assumptions based on names or accents. Discrimination claims can lead to uncapped compensation.
